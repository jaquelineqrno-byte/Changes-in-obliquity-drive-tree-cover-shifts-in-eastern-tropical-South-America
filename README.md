Changes in Earth's obliquity (axial tilt) act as a driver of long-term tree cover shifts in eastern tropical South America by modulating the length of the dry season (DSL). 
High obliquity (maximum tilt) corresponds to decreased dry season length, favoring the expansion of tree cover, while low obliquity (minimum tilt) increases the dry season, leading to the expansion of savanna and grassland.

The duration of the dry season is the main control on the long-term distribution of forests versus savanna/grassland in the São Francisco River drainage basin.
Periods of high obliquity cause greater hemispheric temperature contrast (summer heating/winter cooling), which shifts the Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) further into the southern hemisphere, inducing higher autumn precipitation.
During maximum obliquity, this reduced dry season allows forest-dominated vegetation to expand, while minimum obliquity hampers it, leading to increased grasses.
Obliquity works in conjunction with millennial-scale precipitation changes, such as Heinrich Stadials, which further influence moisture availability, though obliquity provides the underlying, long-term pattern.
